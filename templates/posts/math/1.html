Pascal's Triangle and Lorenz Attractors

How Probability and Python Can Create Something Magnificent

Introduction

As any student learns, mathematics can either be algebraic or geometric. An algebraic equation is one that does not relate a current term with its previous term. The independent variable is related only to the independent variable. However, a geometric sequence is one in which each dependent variable is dependent on the previous dependent variables. An example of this is the fibonacci series, in which each term is the sum of the two terms before it. A common property of geometric sequences is their relationship to fractals, or infinitely recursive images. Take pascal's triangle, for example. If I were to go into every property of pascal's triangle, this would be a much longer page, however, one interesting way to create pascal's triangle is a halving algorithm.

The Algorithm

To better understand this algorithm, refer to the black canvas below. The instructions are very simple. Pick three random points on the canvas to form a triangle. These will be our "anchor" points. For now, keep the increment number at one to understand the algorithm. Proceed by clicking on the canvas. There will be random dots being placed in seemingly random places. The way these dots are being placed is by the halving algorithm. Each time you click, the algorithm chooses two points, one of which is a random anchor point (from the original three points). The other is the current state point. The first time you click, this current position is another random anchor point, however, each other iteration is the dot that was just placed.

The algorithm then proceeds to place another point (that will be our next current point) halfway between the current point and the random anchor point. You can keep placing dots on the canvas, however, if we increase the increment count by entering a valid number in the input form, we can speed this process up. (The only difference is the program does the halving algorithm n times where n is the increment number.) Go ahead an enter a new number. I encourage you to work your way up, so start at 1, then go to 100, then 500, then 1000. From there the pattern seems very clear. It begins to form a very recognizable triangle.

The program is relatively instantaneous up to about 10000. 100000 takes about 3 seconds and 1000000 takes about 15 seconds to compute. To reset, just click the go button after you have entered a valid number.

This algorithm is a geometric, or recursive one. Every point is dependent on the point before it. There would be no way to place these dots non-sequentially like we could with an algebraic formula. Instead, we must place each dot one by one, one after the other.

Other Algorithms

This method of placing dots in places according to previous positions and probability is a very common way to make interesting fractals. With enough time and excitement for fractals, a math student may find themselves inventing other rules based on probability and chance. However, here are some more fun patterns.

Barnsley's Fern

Another very fascinating pattern exists by using the rules of matrices and transformations. Barnsley's fern is a pattern found when you do one of four things with each iteration. You have an x coordinate and a y coordinate and every iteration, you change your x, y coordinate pair by one of the four following transformations. (The formulas are courtesy of wikipedia).

F1 is undergone with a 1% chance, f2 with an 85% chance, f3 with a 7% chance and f4 with a 7% chance. To form barnsley's fern, the coordinate pair (x, y) starts at the position (0, 0). Each iteration, you undergo one of these translations based on their respective probabilities and place a point at the resulting (x, y) coordinate pair. Lorenz Attractor

A more advanced fractal, the Lorenz Attractor, is formed by using a system of linearly homogeneous ordinary differential equations like the ones below.

I won't go into much detail on how the system works. Essentially, The equations describe a fluid that is heated from underneath and cooled from above. x is the rate of convection (so the derivative of x is the second derivative of convection), y is the horizontal temperature difference and z is the vertical temperature difference. Sigma rho and beta are physical constants that describe the Prandtl number, Rayleigh number and some other physical constants. These are some examples and generally, the Lorenz attractor can describe many very interesting fundamental processes.

Conclusion

As a mathematician, fractals have always intrigued me. Although they often have very simple rules, you can create many extremely intricate and detailed patterns which makes them almost artistic. They are so easy to create as well. Give it a little bit of time and a knowledge of recursion and anybody can make any arbitrary rules that turn into something complex and intricate.
